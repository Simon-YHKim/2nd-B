# Framework: Growth Mindset (Dweck)

> Batch produced via `docs/research/psychology-handoff.md`. All DOIs verified against Crossref / publisher record (May 2026).

## Foundational Sources

1. Dweck, C. S., & Leggett, E. L. (1988). A social-cognitive approach to motivation and personality. *Psychological Review*, 95(2), 256–273. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.95.2.256

**Note**: Dweck's trade book *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* (2006, Random House) popularized the framework but is not a peer-reviewed source.

## Recent Validation (last 10 years)

1. Yeager, D. S., & Dweck, C. S. (2020). What can be learned from growth mindset controversies? *American Psychologist*, 75(9), 1269–1284. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/amp0000794
2. Macnamara, B. N., & Burgoyne, A. P. (2023). Do growth mindset interventions impact students' academic achievement? A systematic review and meta-analysis with recommendations for best practices. *Psychological Bulletin*, 149(3–4), 133–173. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/bul0000352

**Note**: Macnamara & Burgoyne is the principal recent critique. After publication-bias correction, intervention effects on academic achievement were non-significant (d ≈ 0.02–0.05). Yeager & Dweck defend the construct citing heterogeneity of effects. A balanced application uses growth mindset as a **disposition** (how one frames learning) while being cautious about claiming interventions reliably change outcomes.

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: applicable — original studies were on schoolchildren; implicit theories about intelligence develop in middle childhood.
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: applicable — most-tested context (academic achievement studies).
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: applicable — extends to college/work domains.
- **Adult (30–49)**: applicable — work skill development, parenting.
- **Midlife (50–64)**: applicable but less studied empirically.
- **Elderly (65+)**: applicable in cognitive engagement / lifelong learning contexts; limited specific evidence.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

Anchored on the entity-vs-incremental theory distinction (Dweck & Leggett 1988). **Reflection prompts, not measure scoring.**

**Korean**
- 본인이 잘 못하는 영역이 있을 때, 그게 ''내가 원래 그런 사람이라서''라고 느끼시나요, 아니면 ''아직 충분히 안 해봐서''라고 느끼시나요? (Entity vs Incremental, ability domain)
- 가장 최근에 실패했다고 느낀 일이 있다면, 그 일에서 본인이 배운 게 있다고 생각하세요? 있다면 어떤 거예요? (Failure framing)
- 누군가 본인보다 훨씬 잘하는 사람을 봤을 때 어떤 생각이 먼저 드세요? (Comparison response)
- 어떤 분야에서 ''나는 안 되겠다''고 일찍 포기해본 경험이 있다면 어떤 거였어요? (Helpless pattern)

**English**
- When you're not good at something, do you tend to think "that's just who I am," or "I haven't worked at it enough yet"? (Entity vs Incremental, ability domain)
- Think of a recent failure — what did you learn from it, if anything? (Failure framing)
- When you see someone who's much better than you at something, what's the first thought that comes up? (Comparison response)
- Tell me about a domain where you gave up early because you decided "I'm just not cut out for it." (Helpless pattern)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **Fixed-mindset (entity)**: trait-attribution language ("I'm not a math person", "I'm just bad at"); avoidance of effortful challenges; threat response to comparison.
- **Growth-mindset (incremental)**: process language ("I'm getting better at"); engagement with hard tasks; learning narratives in failure descriptions.
- **Helpless pattern (Dweck & Leggett 1988)**: rapid affect collapse, attribution of failure to stable ability, withdrawal from task.
- **Mastery-oriented pattern**: process focus during difficulty, sustained engagement, hypothesis-testing problem-solving.

Mindset can be domain-specific — a user can be growth-oriented about cooking and entity about math. Tag by domain, not globally.

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- Use mindset framing **descriptively**, not as an intervention. Macnamara & Burgoyne (2023) shows intervention effects are weak after bias correction; do not promise that mindset shift will improve outcomes.
- For users with entity-mindset patterns: notice the language ("you said you're 'not a writing person' — that came up about photography last month too — what would change if those felt like in-progress skills?"). Reflection, not coaching slogans.
- Avoid "just believe you can grow" framing — Yeager & Dweck (2020) explicitly note that mindset alone without supportive context (teacher behavior, opportunity structure) does not produce change. The Advisor should similarly avoid implying internal mindset shift will solve external structural problems.
- **Do not pathologize fixed mindset**: it is a heuristic, often well-calibrated to actual constraints. The goal is awareness of pattern, not conversion.

## Cautions & Limitations

- **Effect sizes are small and contested**: Macnamara & Burgoyne (2023) meta-analysis of 63 studies (N≈97,000) found d ≈ 0.05 overall, non-significant after publication-bias correction. Highest-quality studies showed d ≈ 0.02. Yeager & Dweck (2020) defend heterogeneity but the contested status must be transparent.
- **Cultural and structural caveats**: original studies were predominantly US-based; applying growth-mindset framing to users facing structural barriers (poverty, discrimination, opportunity scarcity) can shift blame from systems to individuals — a documented harm pattern in the critique literature.
- **Domain specificity**: people often hold different theories for different abilities. Avoid global "this user has fixed mindset" labels.
- **Not a substitute for skill-building**: mindset shifts without skill-building rarely change outcomes. Advisor should not imply otherwise.
- **Korean context unconfirmed**: no DOI-verified Korean validation in this batch — translation-only application is provisional.
