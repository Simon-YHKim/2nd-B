# Framework: Dual-Process Theory (System 1 / System 2 — Cognitive Style)

> Batch produced via `docs/research/psychology-handoff.md`. All DOIs verified against Crossref / publisher record (May 2026).
>
> **Purpose for 2nd-Brain v0.2**: anchor the "cognitive style" dimension (intuitive ↔ analytical) in the peer-reviewed literature, **not in Kahneman's popular book**. Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011, FSG) is a trade book without a single-paper DOI; it summarizes academic work for general readers and is **not the primary citable source**. The citations below are the academic stack the book is built on, plus the modern critiques 2nd-Brain must engage honestly.
>
> **Framing**: dual-process is a useful *cognitive-style* frame for journaling-text inference (does this user lean intuitive or analytical?). It is **not** a validated capacity taxonomy and must not be presented as one.

## Foundational Sources (academic, not the popular book)

1. Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. *Science*, 185(4157), 1124–1131. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124
   - **The origin paper** for the heuristics-and-biases program. Predates dual-process labelling but establishes the empirical floor: people use cognitive shortcuts (representativeness, availability, anchoring) that systematically diverge from normative rules. All later dual-process theorizing builds on this evidence base.

2. Cacioppo, J. T., & Petty, R. E. (1982). The need for cognition. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 42(1), 116–131. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.42.1.116
   - **The dispositional/individual-difference anchor**. Introduces Need for Cognition (NFC) — the stable tendency to engage in and enjoy effortful thinking. This is the construct closest to what 2nd-Brain actually wants to infer from journal text: not a capacity score, but a *style preference*.

3. Sloman, S. A. (1996). The empirical case for two systems of reasoning. *Psychological Bulletin*, 119(1), 3–22. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.119.1.3
   - **The first systematic empirical case** for two qualitatively distinct reasoning systems (associative vs rule-based). Pre-dates Stanovich/Evans labeling as "System 1 / System 2" but is the analytic philosophy spine that makes the later dual-process claim defensible.

4. Stanovich, K. E., & West, R. F. (2000). Individual differences in reasoning: Implications for the rationality debate? *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 23(5), 645–665. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X00003435
   - **Where "System 1" and "System 2" enter the literature as terms.** Stanovich & West formalize the distinction (later popularized by Kahneman) and ground it in individual-differences data. The crucial finding for 2nd-Brain: *people differ stably in their tendency to override System-1 outputs*, and this difference correlates with cognitive ability, thinking dispositions, and real-world judgment quality.

5. Kahneman, D., & Frederick, S. (2002). Representativeness revisited: Attribute substitution in intuitive judgment. In T. Gilovich, D. Griffin, & D. Kahneman (Eds.), *Heuristics and biases: The psychology of intuitive judgment* (pp. 49–81). Cambridge University Press. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511808098.004
   - **The mechanism paper**. Introduces *attribute substitution*: when asked a hard question, System 1 silently substitutes an easier one and answers that. This is the cleanest model of how intuitive judgment fails — directly relevant to journaling, where users often "answer" deep questions about themselves via easier proxy questions ("Am I happy?" → substituted with "How do I feel right now?").

6. Kahneman, D. (2003). A perspective on judgment and choice: Mapping bounded rationality. *American Psychologist*, 58(9), 697–720. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.58.9.697
   - **Kahneman's own academic synthesis** (Nobel address adaptation), eight years before the popular book. This is the *citable* version of the System 1 / System 2 model — accessibility, effort, monitoring failures. **Cite this, not the trade book.**

7. Evans, J. St. B. T. (2008). Dual-processing accounts of reasoning, judgment, and social cognition. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 59, 255–278. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.psych.59.103006.093629
   - **The Annual Review consolidation**. Maps the entire dual-process family (across reasoning, judgment, social cognition, learning) and clarifies that "dual-process" is not one theory but a family of related models. Important caveat for 2nd-Brain: do not over-unify these as "System 1 / System 2" — that's one labeling choice among several.

## Recent Validation and Theoretical Refinement

8. Frederick, S. (2005). Cognitive reflection and decision making. *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 19(4), 25–42. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1257/089533005775196732
   - **The Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT)** — the most-used 3-item behavioral measure of disposition to override intuitive responses. The "bat-and-ball" item lives here. CRT correlates moderately with general cognitive ability but adds independent variance for predicting heuristics-and-biases performance, patience in intertemporal choice, and risk preferences.

9. Toplak, M. E., West, R. F., & Stanovich, K. E. (2011). The Cognitive Reflection Test as a predictor of performance on heuristics-and-biases tasks. *Memory & Cognition*, 39(7), 1275–1289. DOI: https://doi.org/10.3758/s13421-011-0104-1
   - **CRT incremental-validity evidence**. Across two studies CRT predicted heuristics-and-biases task performance *over and above* intelligence, executive function, and thinking dispositions — establishing that CRT measures something distinct (the *willingness* to engage analytic processing, not raw capacity).

10. Toplak, M. E., West, R. F., & Stanovich, K. E. (2014). Assessing miserly information processing: An expansion of the Cognitive Reflection Test. *Thinking & Reasoning*, 20(2), 147–168. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/13546783.2013.844729
    - **CRT-2**: a 4-item extension reducing math/numeracy confound. Useful methodologically — confirms that what CRT measures isn't math ability but *cognitive miserliness* — the tendency to accept the first answer System 1 supplies without checking.

11. Pennycook, G., Fugelsang, J. A., & Koehler, D. J. (2015). Everyday consequences of analytic thinking. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 24(6), 425–432. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721415604610
    - **The "what does analytic style predict?" anchor**. Reviews evidence that analytic cognitive style predicts religious disbelief, paranormal skepticism, moral judgment patterns, and political attitudes — independently of intelligence. Direct relevance for 2nd-Brain: cognitive style isn't an isolated trait, it shapes how users describe values, beliefs, and choices in journal text.

12. Otero, I., Salgado, J. F., & Moscoso, S. (2021). Criterion validity of cognitive reflection for predicting job performance and training proficiency: A meta-analysis. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 12, 668592. DOI: https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.668592
    - **Meta-analytic validity for CRT.** Establishes CRT predicts job performance and training outcomes with effect sizes comparable to general cognitive ability tests. Cited here only as evidence that analytic-thinking disposition has real-world consequences — not as endorsement of using CRT inside 2nd-Brain (we don't administer tests).

## Major Critiques (must be included for honest framing)

13. Keren, G., & Schul, Y. (2009). Two is not always better than one: A critical evaluation of two-system theories. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 4(6), 533–550. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-6924.2009.01164.x
    - **The first major critique** from inside cognitive science. Argues that the "two systems" framing is under-specified: the proposed dichotomies (fast/slow, automatic/controlled, associative/rule-based, evolutionary-old/new) don't cleanly co-vary. Two-systems theorizing risks becoming unfalsifiable.

14. Evans, J. St. B. T., & Stanovich, K. E. (2013). Dual-process theories of higher cognition: Advancing the debate. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 8(3), 223–241. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691612460685
    - **The defenders' response** to early critiques. Concedes that many of the original "System 1 / System 2" attribute clusters were misleading; argues the *core* distinction (Type 1 = autonomous, Type 2 = working-memory-dependent) survives. Crucially: they explicitly abandon the strict "two systems" framing and prefer "Type 1 / Type 2 processing" — a softer claim about *processes*, not architectural modules.

15. Melnikoff, D. E., & Bargh, J. A. (2018). The mythical number two. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 22(4), 280–293. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tics.2018.02.001
    - **The most consequential modern critique.** Argues the four "core" dichotomies underlying dual-process theory (unintentional/intentional, uncontrollable/controllable, autonomous/cognitive-load-sensitive, unconscious/conscious) do not empirically co-vary as the theory predicts. Conclusion: there is no scientifically meaningful "two" — the binary is a folk-psychological abstraction that has outlived its usefulness as a research framework.

16. De Neys, W. (2021). On dual- and single-process models of thinking. *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 16(6), 1412–1427. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691620964172
    - **The current-state-of-debate synthesis.** Reviews evidence against the simple "System 1 fast & wrong, System 2 slow & right" picture: people often *intuit the correct (logical/probabilistic) answer first* before deliberation. Proposes single-process and hybrid models. **This is the citation that prevents 2nd-Brain from over-promising the dual-process frame.**

## Cross-Cultural & Korean-Context Anchors

17. Nisbett, R. E., Peng, K., Choi, I., & Norenzayan, A. (2001). Culture and systems of thought: Holistic versus analytic cognition. *Psychological Review*, 108(2), 291–310. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.108.2.291
    - **The cross-cultural baseline.** Documents systematic East Asian (holistic, context-dependent, dialectical) vs Western (analytic, rule-based, formal-logical) cognition differences. For 2nd-Brain Korean users this is the *prerequisite framing*: the "System 1 vs System 2" dichotomy is itself a Western analytic-cognition product. Holistic thinking is not "less System 2" — it is a different reasoning mode.

18. Choi, I., Koo, M., & Choi, J. A. (2007). Individual differences in analytic versus holistic thinking. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 33(5), 691–705. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167206298568
    - **The Analysis-Holism Scale (AHS)** — a 24-item validated measure of analytic ↔ holistic cognitive style, constructed and validated across American and Korean samples. Koreans and Korean-medicine students score higher than Americans on holism. This is **the closest validated Korean cognitive-style instrument** and is *more culturally appropriate* for Korean users than CRT-style measures.

19. Buchtel, E. E., & Norenzayan, A. (2009). Thinking across cultures: Implications for dual processes. In J. St. B. T. Evans & K. Frankish (Eds.), *In two minds: Dual processes and beyond* (Chapter 10). Oxford University Press. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199230167.003.0010
    - **The bridge chapter** linking cultural-psychology holistic/analytic distinction to dual-process theory. Argues that what Western dual-process research calls "intuitive" maps imperfectly onto East Asian "holistic" — they overlap but aren't identical. Holistic thinking can be both fast *and* normatively appropriate in many contexts (e.g., relational reasoning).

## Need for Cognition (the related, more-validated dispositional construct)

20. Cacioppo, J. T., Petty, R. E., Feinstein, J. A., & Jarvis, W. B. G. (1996). Dispositional differences in cognitive motivation: The life and times of individuals varying in need for cognition. *Psychological Bulletin*, 119(2), 197–253. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.119.2.197
    - **The NFC review** synthesizing ~100 studies. NFC is a more empirically secure construct than "System 1 vs System 2 person": it's a continuous individual difference, not a binary, and predicts attitude formation, persuasion susceptibility, information search, and decision quality. **For 2nd-Brain, NFC is the *safer* construct to anchor cognitive-style inference on than System 1/2.**

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: not applicable as cognitive-style frame for 2nd-Brain (not our user base; CRT and NFC measures are adult-validated).
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: applicable; CRT and NFC validated in adolescents but high developmental variance in working memory confounds inference. Not 2nd-Brain user base.
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: most-studied range — Frederick (2005) original sample, Toplak studies, Pennycook studies all heavily university-student-based.
- **Adult (30–49)**: applicable. NFC stable across this range.
- **Midlife (50–64)**: applicable, with cognitive-aging caveats — System 2 / deliberate processing slows with age but disposition (NFC) is relatively stable.
- **Elderly (65+)**: applicable for *style* but not for *capacity* inference — processing speed declines confound CRT-style measures. Stick to NFC-style dispositional inference for older users.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Where dual-process belongs in the Engine pipeline

- **Engine 2 (Inference) — cognitive-style dimension**: use as a *style* axis (intuitive ↔ analytical), **not** a capacity score. Combine with Big Five Openness (curiosity-driven reflection) and Conscientiousness (deliberate planning) for triangulation.
- **Engine 3 (Persona Card)**: display as *preference description*, never as type label. "You tend to reason through decisions step-by-step" is acceptable; "You are a System 2 thinker" is not.
- **Engine 4 (Advisor)**: tune question framing to user's inferred style — more "what does your gut say?" for intuitive-leaning users, more "let's lay out the trade-offs" for analytic-leaning users. **Always offer the other mode as well** — the goal is meta-awareness, not reinforcement of one style.
- **Engine 7 (Safety)**: dual-process frame does *not* apply to RED zone content. Crisis routing supersedes — see `crisis-detection.md`.

### Interview Question Examples (validated framing, not validated as instruments)

These are journaling prompts inspired by NFC, CRT logic, and Pennycook's everyday-analytic-thinking work — adapted as reflection cues. **They are not validated assessments.**

**Korean — surfacing intuitive style**
- 오늘 결정한 일들 중에서 깊이 생각 안 하고 그냥 느낌대로 선택한 게 있다면 어떤 거였어요? (Intuitive-mode awareness)
- 처음 든 느낌과 끝까지 갔던 결론이 같았어요, 달랐어요? (Attribute-substitution awareness — Kahneman & Frederick 2002)
- 이런 종류의 결정은 본인한테 보통 빠르게 와요, 천천히 와요? (Style self-observation)

**Korean — surfacing analytical style**
- 요즘 머릿속에서 어떤 일을 자꾸 여러 각도로 따져보고 있으세요? (Deliberation-mode awareness; not assumed to be "better")
- 그렇게 따져본 게 결정에 도움이 됐어요, 아니면 더 막막해졌어요? (Cognitive-miserliness check inverse — does deliberation pay off here?)
- 비슷한 결정을 누군가 옆에서 본다면 본인이 너무 많이 생각한다고 할까요, 충분히 생각 안 한다고 할까요? (Outside-view check)

**English — surfacing intuitive style**
- What did you decide today on feel, without thinking it through? (Intuitive-mode awareness)
- Did your first sense of it match where you ended up, or did it shift? (Substitution awareness)
- Does this kind of choice usually come to you fast or slow?

**English — surfacing analytical style**
- What have you been turning over from multiple angles lately?
- Did the working-through clarify it, or make it murkier? (Inverse miserliness check)
- If someone watched how you made this decision, would they say you over-thought it or under-thought it?

### Trait Extraction Cues (linguistic markers in journal text)

These are *heuristic* markers for cognitive-style inference, not validated text classifiers. Use with confidence bands per `computational-personality.md` (Park 2015 r≈0.4 ceiling applies here too).

- **Intuitive-leaning markers**: feeling words preceding decisions ("felt right", "just knew", "gut said"), absence of trade-off language, low frequency of conditional/probabilistic terms ("if", "probably", "depends"), single-frame descriptions of choices.
- **Analytic-leaning markers**: explicit pro/con structure, conditional and probabilistic terms, hedging ("on the one hand…on the other"), counterfactuals ("if I had…"), explicit consideration of alternatives, numeric or comparative language, longer decision narratives.
- **Cognitive-miserliness markers** (Toplak et al. 2014): self-reported quick conclusions paired with subsequent regret, repeated reversals on the same decision, abandonment of effortful planning under stress.
- **High-NFC markers** (Cacioppo et al. 1996): expressed enjoyment of complex problems, voluntary engagement with abstract topics in entries, vocabulary diversity around reasoning processes.
- **Holistic-leaning markers** (Choi, Koo & Choi 2007, especially for Korean users): context-sensitive descriptions, attention to relationships and situational factors, dialectical statements ("both X and not-X"), absence of strict either/or framing.

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- **Style-aware, not style-prescriptive**: surface the user's inferred style as observation, not destiny. "You seem to reach decisions quickly — does that serve you, or do you wish you took more time on some?"
- **Productize the Kahneman & Frederick (2002) substitution insight**: when a user repeatedly answers a hard question (e.g., "Am I living the right life?") via easy proxies (e.g., "I had a good day today"), gently surface the gap.
- **Never recommend "be more System 2"**: the De Neys (2021) and Melnikoff & Bargh (2018) literature is clear that *more deliberation is not always better*. Many domains (interpersonal, aesthetic, ethical) reward intuitive processing; many analytic users *over-deliberate to paralysis*. Match the recommendation to the domain, not to a generic "think more carefully" script.
- **For Korean users specifically**: anchor style inference in Choi/Koo/Choi (2007) AHS dimensions (analytic ↔ holistic) before applying Western intuitive/analytic frame. Holistic reasoning is a culturally-normative *strength* in Korean context, not a deficit relative to analytic.
- **Cross-reference Big Five Openness and Conscientiousness** (`big-five.md`): cognitive-style inference is more robust when triangulated with Openness (curiosity, reflection) and Conscientiousness (deliberateness) rather than standing alone.
- **Cross-reference reflection vs rumination** (`self-knowledge.md`): high deliberation can be productive Type-2 reasoning *or* rumination wearing analytic clothing. The reflection/rumination distinction supersedes the System 1/2 frame in mental-health-adjacent territory.

## Cautions & Limitations

- **Dual-process is a useful heuristic frame, not a fully-validated taxonomy.** Treat it as a *cognitive-style descriptor*, not a discovered cognitive architecture.
- **The popular book *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Kahneman, 2011, FSG) is not a primary academic source.** It is a trade-press synthesis. The citable evidence is Kahneman (2003) and the Stanovich/Evans/Frederick stack above. Never cite the book as scientific evidence in user-facing copy or product documentation.
- **Recent critique questions the binary itself.** Melnikoff & Bargh (2018) and De Neys (2021) show the proposed "two-system" attribute clusters don't co-vary as the theory requires. Do not present dual-process as settled science.
- **Use as *cognitive style*, not *cognitive capacity*.** Style = preferred mode of engagement (NFC-style, dispositional). Capacity = working memory, reasoning skill (IQ-style). 2nd-Brain has zero business inferring the latter from journal text.
- **No type labels.** Do not output "you are a System 1 / System 2 / intuitive / analytical type." Use continuous descriptive language ("you tend to…", "you often…") with explicit invitation to disagree (see `computational-personality.md` confidence-band rules).
- **Cultural validity.** The intuitive/analytic frame was developed in Western (largely WEIRD) samples. For Korean users, the Choi/Koo/Choi (2007) Analysis-Holism distinction is more culturally calibrated. Holistic thinking is not "Type 1 / impulsive" — it is a different reasoning mode entirely.
- **CRT is not used inside 2nd-Brain.** It is a 3-item test; we don't administer tests in-product. CRT is cited only as evidence that cognitive-miserliness is a measurable individual difference, justifying that we *can* infer style from text.
- **Cognitive-style inference accuracy ceiling.** Per `computational-personality.md` Park 2015, text-based personality inference tops out around r ≈ 0.4. Cognitive-style inference is likely no better. Show confidence bands; invite disagreement.
- **Not therapy, not diagnosis.** Dual-process is descriptive psychology of judgment, not psychopathology. Cognitive style is not a clinical category. Per `ai-mental-health-safety.md`, no diagnostic claims.
- **Stress/load distorts the signal.** Acute stress shifts everyone toward Type-1 / heuristic processing (Kahneman 2003). A single stressful entry is not evidence of stable intuitive style. Aggregate over weeks before making style claims.
