# Framework: Cross-Cultural & East Asian Cultural Psychology (Korean / Japanese / Confucian constructs)

> Batch produced via `docs/research/psychology-handoff.md`. All DOIs verified against Crossref / publisher record (May 2026).
>
> **Why this batch exists**: 2nd-Brain v0.2 design establishes that the user's declared nationality/culture takes **priority** in theory application (Tier 5 in `psychology-handoff.md`). This batch supplies the *empirical* cultural-psychology layer that should weight before — or be triangulated with — the Western frameworks already in `big-five.md`, `attachment.md`, `sdt.md`, etc., when the user is Korean, Japanese, or East-Asian-diasporic.
>
> **What this batch is NOT**: a celebration of "uniquely Korean / Japanese" essences. The dominant finding from 50 years of cross-cultural psychology (Oyserman 2002 meta-analysis, Schimmack 2005) is that **within-culture variance is larger than between-culture variance** on most dimensions. Treat every construct below as a *probability weighting* over the user's likely repertoire, not as a deterministic profile. See `Cautions & Limitations`.

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: partially applicable — Korean / Japanese family-system constructs (우리, amae) shape early attachment, but the empirical literature in this batch is overwhelmingly adult-focused. For child-stage Korean users defer to `attachment.md` (Strange Situation cross-cultural caveats) and `erikson.md`.
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: applicable — identity formation under bicultural pressure (Kang 2013 on diaspora; Benet-Martínez 2005 BII). Chemyon and nunchi become salient around peer hierarchy.
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: applicable — primary window for Suh (2002) identity-consistency findings, BII research, jeong / we-ness operating in romantic and workplace relationships.
- **Adult (30–49)**: applicable — Confucian relational obligations (eldercare, generativity in family hierarchy) intensify. Hwang (1987, 2001) on face-and-favor exchanges most operative here.
- **Midlife (50–64)**: applicable — han narratives more common in this cohort (Boman 2020 implicitly, since his film cases center midlife loss); intergenerational tatemae most salient.
- **Elderly (65+)**: applicable — life-review narratives in Korean elderly often invoke han, jeong, and intergenerational debt; see `narrative-identity.md` Park et al. (2024) Korean TALE for the measurement bridge.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### When user profile = KR (Korean, monocultural)

**Weight order** (highest first, but never deterministic):
1. We-ness / uri framing of close relationships (Choi & Kim 2006) — assume in-group/out-group axis is psychologically active before assuming Western dyadic-attachment frame
2. Jeong (Choi & Choi 2001; Son 2014) — recognize "끈끈하다", "정 들었다" as bonded-relationship signals, not codependence
3. Chemyon (Kim & Yang 2011) — distinguish *social* chemyon (performance, status) from *personal* chemyon (closer to Western face); social chemyon explicitly correlates with avoidant conflict styles
4. Nunchi (Kim 2026) — treat as social-perception skill, NOT as obligation to suppress own needs; the 2026 cross-cultural validation finds nunchi has both adaptive (sensitivity) and maladaptive (over-monitoring) dimensions
5. Han (Boman 2020) — only invoke if user surfaces it themselves; never AI-impose han framing (essentialism risk)

Then layer Western frameworks (Big Five, attachment, SDT) as triangulating signal, not as primary lens.

### When user profile = JP (Japanese, monocultural)

1. Amae (Doi 1962; Niiya 2006; Yamaguchi 2004) — recognize comfortable-dependence behaviors as relationship-affirming, not regressive; Niiya 2006 specifically shows amae expectations differ from US "dependence" connotations
2. Honne / tatemae (Bachnik 2007) — interpret journal entries showing public/private divergence as functional, not as hypocrisy or inauthenticity
3. Low-arousal positive affect ideal (Tsai 2006) — calm/peaceful states are *culturally ideal*; do NOT push high-energy "thriving" framings
4. Heine 1999 caveat — do NOT push self-esteem-enhancement interventions; Japanese self-improvement orientation is critical-rather-than-positive

### When user profile = bi-cultural (Korean-American / Korean-Chinese / Korean-Japanese / diaspora)

- Apply Benet-Martínez & Haritatos (2005) BII frame: assess whether user reports bicultural identity as **blended** (high BII) or **conflicted** (low BII). Low-BII users benefit from validation of dual-track identity rather than integration pressure.
- Berry (1997, 2005) acculturation strategies — Integration / Assimilation / Separation / Marginalization. Surface which the user is reporting; do not assign.
- Suh (2002) — for bicultural users, identity *inconsistency across cultural contexts* may be adaptive, not pathological. Resist the Western "authentic self" narrative.
- Kang (2013) — the "not X, not Y" framing is itself a stable identity position; honor it.

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

**Korean (KR profile)**
- 최근에 누군가에게 정이 들었다거나, 또는 정이 떨어졌다고 느낀 순간이 있나요? 어떤 순간이었는지 떠올려 봐 주세요. (Choi & Choi 2001 jeong-as-process)
- "우리 식구" 또는 "우리 팀"이라고 부르는 사람들 안에서, 본인 위치는 어떤 것 같아요? (Choi & Kim 2006 we-ness operationalization)
- 다른 사람들 앞에서 보여지는 본인과, 혼자 있을 때의 본인이 다를 때가 있어요? 그게 부담스러운가요, 자연스러운가요? (Kim & Yang 2011 chemyon — note: "부담 vs 자연스러움" framing avoids implying dishonesty)
- 어떤 자리에서 분위기를 빨리 읽어야 한다고 느낄 때, 어떤 신호들을 보세요? (Kim 2026 nunchi — frame as skill, not obligation)
- (only if user surfaces it) "한"이라는 말을 본인 삶에 쓴다면, 어떤 경험이 떠올라요? (Boman 2020 — user-initiated only)

**Korean (bicultural)**
- 한국 문화권에 있을 때와 다른 문화권에 있을 때, 본인의 어떤 면이 더 자연스럽게 나오나요? (Suh 2002 identity-across-contexts; Benet-Martínez 2005 BII)
- "둘 다 아닌 것 같은" 순간이 있다면 언제예요? 그게 답답한가요, 아니면 그게 본인다운 거예요? (Kang 2013 — validates "neither" as identity)

**Japanese (JP profile)**
- 誰かに甘えたくなった、または甘えたくないと感じた最近の状況を教えてください。(Doi 1962; Niiya 2006 amae)
- 本音と建前を使い分けたほうがよかった場面と、本音を出しすぎて疲れた場面、両方思い浮かびますか? (Bachnik 2007)
- 「穏やかで落ち着いた」気持ちと「興奮して元気な」気持ち、どちらに近い状態が理想ですか? (Tsai 2006 low-arousal positive affect)

**English (any East-Asian or diaspora user)**
- Tell me about a relationship where the bond is more "ours" than just "yours and mine" — what does that feel like? (we-ness; jeong)
- When you sense the room before saying anything — is that something you choose to do, or is it automatic? (nunchi)
- Are there parts of you that only show up around certain people or in certain languages? (Suh 2002; Kang 2013; BII)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **Strong we-ness (uri) operative**: pronouns "우리" / "我々" applied to family, workplace, religious community; goals framed as "our family's goal"; reluctance to articulate individual preference without group context. Do NOT code as low autonomy on SDT until checking whether the user *experiences* this as constraint vs identity.
- **Jeong-bonded relationships**: explicit "정 들었다 / 정이 깊다" language; willingness to inconvenience self for relationship continuity; difficulty leaving relationships even when the user reports them as costly. Do NOT pathologize as anxious attachment without checking Korean cultural baseline.
- **High social chemyon**: heightened concern with how performance is perceived by status superiors; conflict avoidance in face-threat situations (Kim & Yang 2011). Tag separately from generalized social anxiety.
- **Amae behaviors**: described episodes of comfortable expectation that an intimate other will tolerate the user's bad mood / inconvenience / non-explicit request. Niiya 2006 shows Japanese participants rate these episodes as positive; do NOT code as immature dependency.
- **Honne/tatemae divergence**: user reports systematic difference between public expression and private feeling without distress about it. Bachnik 2007: this is functional in Japanese contexts; flag as concern only if user *itself* reports the divergence as costly.
- **Han markers**: user-initiated invocation of historical/generational sorrow, unspeakable accumulated loss. Never AI-impose; honor when surfaced.
- **Low-BII bicultural conflict**: explicit reports of "switching" between cultural selves with effort, dissonance between cultural expectations. Validate; do not push toward integration as a goal.

Aggregate across ≥20 entries for cultural-construct tagging (higher threshold than Western constructs because surface-level cultural language can be borrowed without deep operation).

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- **Default**: ask the user how their cultural context interacts with the issue before applying Western framework. Phrase: "한국 문화권에서 [상황]을 어떻게 받아들이는지 먼저 듣고 싶어요" / "Before I suggest anything, how does the Korean / Japanese context shape this for you?"
- **Never assert cultural belonging**: do not tell a Korean user "as a Korean, you probably feel X". Always offer as hypothesis the user can reject. Phrase: "이게 한국 문화권에서 흔한 패턴이긴 한데, 본인한테도 그렇게 느껴져요?"
- **Resist coherent-self pressure for Korean / Japanese users**: Suh (2002) shows identity consistency across contexts is less predictive of wellbeing in collectivist contexts. Do not push narrative integration as a wellbeing goal for these users; multiple contextual selves are functional.
- **Honor we-ness without coercing autonomy**: SDT autonomy needs (Ryan & Deci) are universal in basic-need form, but their expression is filtered through we-ness. A Korean user choosing in line with family expectation is not necessarily acting under introjected motivation — check the user's *felt* experience (Sheldon & Elliot 1999 self-concordance) rather than the surface structure.
- **For amae behaviors in Japanese users**: validate as relational asset rather than coding as anxious attachment. Niiya 2006 explicitly distinguishes amae from Western anxious dependence.
- **For chemyon / face-management**: respect user's chosen public presentation; do not push toward radical authenticity / disclosure as universal good.
- **For bicultural users**: avoid framing one cultural side as "real self" and the other as "performed". Both are real. Benet-Martínez (2005) BII research finds blendedness is a continuum, not a goal-state.
- **For han**: never AI-introduce. If user invokes, listen without trying to redirect to growth / silver-lining framing (see `narrative-identity.md` redemptive-narrative caution).

## Cautions & Limitations

### 1. Essentialism is the main failure mode

Every construct in this batch has been ESSENTIALIZED in popular and academic discourse — treated as a fixed national/ethnic property of all Koreans/Japanese. The empirical literature does not support this. Specifically:

- **Han** is the most contested. Boman (2020) and the broader literature (Kim 2017 etc., referenced in our research search but not added as a row because not yet DOI-verified) explicitly note that the modern conception of han as a unique Korean essence emerged during/after Japanese colonization (1910–1945) and was retroactively projected as ancient. Treating han as universally present in Korean users is itself a colonial-era construct.
- **Amae** was historically claimed by Doi (1973) as untranslatably Japanese. Niiya, Ellsworth, & Yamaguchi (2006) empirically demonstrate that American participants recognize amae-equivalent emotions when given the situational frame — the construct is not Japan-exclusive, it is differently *valued* and *labeled*.
- **Nunchi** is presented in trade press (Hong 2019) as a Korean superpower. Kim (2026) empirically validates the construct cross-culturally and finds American participants also score on nunchi-equivalent items, again with different cultural valuation.

**Rule**: any construct in this batch must be presented to the user as a hypothesis they can confirm, reject, or modify. Never as established fact about them based on their nationality.

### 2. Within-culture variance > between-culture variance

Oyserman, Coon, & Kemmelmeier (2002) meta-analyzed 170+ studies of individualism / collectivism and found that effect sizes between US and other countries are often smaller than within-US variance. Schimmack, Oishi, & Diener (2005) show that Hofstede's I/C scores are valid only after correcting for response-style artifacts. **Implication**: a Korean user with high individualistic self-construal is NOT a deviation from "Korean essence" — they are within normal Korean range.

### 3. Indigenous-psychology sources are theoretically rich but empirically uneven

The Korean Indigenous Psychology project (최상진, 한규석, 김의철 etc., partially captured by Kim, Park, & Park 1999 and Choi & Kim 2006) has strong conceptual contributions and weaker measurement track records. Many constructs (jeong, uri, chemyon) have only recently begun receiving formal psychometric treatment (e.g., Kim & Yang 2011 chemyon scale; Kim 2026 nunchi scale). Treat indigenous-construct claims as **operationalized hypotheses**, not as established Tier-A psychology.

### 4. Distinguish empirical psychology from cultural anthropology

Several common references in this domain (Lebra 1976; Davies & Ikeno 2002; Hong 2019) are anthropology, popular nonfiction, or trade press — not empirical psychology. They are excluded from `knowledge_sources` rows. Their absence is intentional, not an oversight. Tier policy from `CLAUDE.md` applies: book-only sources without peer-reviewed empirical backing are Tier D (do not cite as scientific evidence).

### 5. Korean / Japanese cultural psychology ≠ all of East Asian cultural psychology

This batch deliberately focuses on Korean and Japanese constructs because they are the priority locales for 2nd-Brain Phase 1. Chinese-mainland constructs (e.g., 关系 guanxi, 面子 mianzi) overlap with Hwang's Confucian framework but are not directly transferable to Korean/Japanese contexts. Do not apply Chinese-specific constructs to Korean or Japanese users without separate verification.

### 6. Diasporic / bicultural users are NOT lesser versions of monocultural users

Berry (1997, 2005) and Benet-Martínez & Haritatos (2005) explicitly establish bicultural identity as a legitimate, often *advantageous* developmental position. Kang (2013) documents the "not Korean, but not American" identity as stable and self-coherent. **Never** push a diasporic user toward "reclaiming" a monocultural identity as if that were more authentic.

### 7. Crisis routing supersedes cultural framing

If a user's content shows crisis markers (self-harm, suicidal ideation, severe loss + acute distress), the cultural-construct layer is **deferred**. Crisis handling per `crisis-detection.md` runs first. Cultural sensitivity is layered into crisis routing via Na et al. (2020) Korean Suicide CARE 2.0 — see that batch.

### 8. Linguistic note

Romanization of Korean constructs is inconsistent across the literature (jeong / cheong / chŏng; chemyon / chaemyon / chemyeon; nunchi / noonchi). The Hangul forms (정, 체면, 눈치, 우리, 한) are the canonical reference. For Japanese constructs (甘え, 本音, 建前, 以心伝心), the kanji are canonical. Store both Hangul/kanji and romanized forms in `knowledge_sources.summary_ko` / `summary_en` to support retrieval regardless of which form the user uses.
