## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

Anchored on Neff's three components: self-kindness vs self-judgment, common humanity vs isolation, mindfulness vs over-identification (Neff 2003). **Reflection prompts only.**

**Korean**
- 본인이 실수했을 때 스스로에게 보통 어떻게 말씀하세요? 친한 친구에게 같은 일이 일어났다면 그 친구에게는 뭐라고 말씀하실 것 같으세요? (Self-kindness vs self-judgment)
- 힘든 일이 생겼을 때, ''나만 이런 일을 겪는 것 같다''는 느낌이 드신 적이 있나요? (Common humanity vs isolation)
- 부정적인 감정이 올라올 때 본인은 그 감정에 ''휩쓸리는'' 편이세요, 아니면 ''관찰하는'' 편이세요? (Mindfulness vs over-identification)

**English**
- When you make a mistake, what do you tend to say to yourself? If a close friend had made the same mistake, what would you say to them? (Self-kindness vs self-judgment)
- When something hard happens, do you ever feel like "I'm the only one this happens to"? (Common humanity vs isolation)
- When difficult emotions show up, do you tend to get swept up in them, or can you observe them with some distance? (Mindfulness vs over-identification)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **Low self-kindness**: harsh self-talk ("I'm so stupid", "I can't believe I"); rumination on personal failings; absent self-soothing language.
- **Self-judgment + isolation pattern**: combining self-criticism with "no one else struggles like this" framing — high MacBeth & Gumley (2012) psychopathology risk signal.
- **Common humanity present**: explicit normalizing of struggle as human ("this is what people go through"), seeking shared experience.
- **Mindfulness present**: emotion-naming with some perspective ("I notice I'm feeling X"), distinct from immersion ("I AM angry/sad").
- **Over-identification**: identity-merge with state ("I'm a failure" not "I failed at X").

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- For users with high self-judgment patterns: use the friend-comparison question (above) as a low-friction reflection — gap between how they treat themselves vs a friend is often striking and self-evident.
- Avoid presenting self-compassion as a "should" or new self-criticism vector ("I'm not good enough at being self-compassionate"). Neff's clinical caveat: a user can weaponize self-compassion into another evaluative dimension.
- Use MacBeth & Gumley (2012) finding to ground the framework: large effect (r = −0.54) between self-compassion and psychopathology indicates this is genuinely protective, not just positive-psychology fluff.
- For Korean users, use Chae et al. (2024) Korean SCS items as the vocabulary basis — direct translation of English SCS items can sound stilted in Korean self-talk contexts.
- **Self-compassion ≠ self-indulgence**: the framework explicitly distinguishes these. Advisor should mirror this distinction when users worry "if I'm kind to myself I'll get lazy."

## Cautions & Limitations

- **SCS factor structure debate**: the original 6-factor / 2-factor (positive vs negative) structure of the SCS has been challenged (e.g., Muris et al. work). Modern researchers often analyze the 3 positive components separately from the 3 negative components. Treat the construct as nuanced, not unitary.
- **Not therapy**: self-compassion practices are not a replacement for treatment of depression, anxiety, trauma, or eating disorders. Route distress to Safety Classifier.
- **Cultural context**: self-compassion was developed in a Western individualist frame. In collectivist contexts (including Korean), the "self vs others" framing of common humanity may translate differently — Chae et al. (2024) note items required cultural adjustment.
- **Risk of bypass**: self-compassion can be used to avoid difficult action (forgiving oneself for repeated harm to others). Pair with accountability frames when relevant.
- **Avoid romanticization**: not all users have equal access to self-compassion — early-attachment patterns and trauma history affect baseline capacity. Patience over prescription.
