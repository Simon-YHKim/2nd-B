# Framework: Cross-Cultural & Global South Indigenous Psychologies (Latin American / African / South Asian / Middle Eastern–Arab)

> Batch produced via `docs/research/psychology-handoff.md`. All DOIs verified against Crossref / publisher record (May 2026).
>
> **Why this batch exists**: 2nd-Brain v0.2 design §06 establishes "user country = 1st priority" for theory selection. The `cross-cultural-east-asian.md` batch covers Korean, Japanese, and Confucian/Chinese constructs. This batch extends the same cultural-priority logic to Latin American (MX/AR/BR/CO/CL etc.), African (NG/KE/ZA/GH etc.), South Asian (IN/PK/BD/LK), and Middle Eastern / Arab (EG/JO/LB/SA/MA etc.) users. Without this batch, 2nd-Brain would default these locales to Western (US/UK) frameworks — exactly what the priority rule was designed to prevent.
>
> **What this batch is NOT**: a region-by-region essentialization. Allwood & Berry (2006), Cheung (2012), and the Hofstede critiques (Oyserman 2002 / Schimmack 2005, already cited in the East-Asian batch) jointly establish that **within-region variance is larger than between-region variance** on most psychological dimensions. Indigenous psychology, as Allwood & Berry define it, is *a methodological commitment to local conceptual grounding*, not a claim of bounded national essences. Every construct below is a **probability weighting** over the user's likely repertoire, with confidence bands explicitly lower than for East-Asian constructs because measurement infrastructure is uneven.
>
> **Confidence band note**: the empirical/psychometric base for Latin American (especially Mexican ethnopsychology) and Arab (Dwairy/San Martin) constructs is moderate-to-strong. The base for African (Ubuntu, Nsamenang) and Indian (dharma/karma/sneh) constructs is **theoretically rich but psychometrically thin** — many constructs are operationalized in only one or two papers. Tag advisor outputs from this batch with lower confidence than from the East-Asian batch by default.

## Foundational Sources

### Indigenous psychology as a discipline

1. Kim, U., Yang, K.-S., & Hwang, K.-K. (Eds.). (2006). *Indigenous and Cultural Psychology: Understanding People in Context*. Springer (International and Cultural Psychology series). Book DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/0-387-28662-4
2. Kim, U., Yang, K.-S., & Hwang, K.-K. (2006). Contributions to indigenous and cultural psychology. In *Indigenous and Cultural Psychology: Understanding People in Context* (Ch. 1). Springer. Chapter DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/0-387-28662-4_1
3. Allwood, C. M., & Berry, J. W. (2006). Origins and development of indigenous psychologies: An international analysis. *International Journal of Psychology*, 41(4), 243–268. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207590544000013
4. Allwood, C. M., & Berry, J. W. (2006). Preface: Special issue on the indigenous psychologies. *International Journal of Psychology*, 41(4), 241–242. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207590544000022
5. Cheung, F. M. (2012). Mainstreaming culture in psychology. *American Psychologist*, 67(8), 721–730. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0029876

**Note on Sinha (1997) "Indigenizing psychology"**: This is a chapter in Berry, J. W. et al. (Eds.), *Handbook of Cross-Cultural Psychology* (Vol. 1: Theory and Method, 2nd ed.), Allyn & Bacon, 1997. Crossref searches return no individual-chapter DOI for this edition. The conceptual content is integrated into the Allwood & Berry (2006) origins paper above and into Bhawuk (2011) "The global need for indigenous psychology" (DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4419-8110-3_1), which is added as the citable empirical anchor for Sinha's indigenizing program. Treat Sinha (1997) as a referenced foundational essay; the DOI-verified anchors above carry its program forward.

### Cross-cultural Big Five validation in Global South samples

6. McCrae, R. R., Terracciano, A., & 79 Members of the Personality Profiles of Cultures Project. (2005). Universal features of personality traits from the observer's perspective: Data from 50 cultures. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 88(3), 547–561. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.88.3.547
7. Schmitt, D. P., Allik, J., McCrae, R. R., & Benet-Martínez, V. (2007). The geographic distribution of Big Five personality traits: Patterns and profiles of human self-description across 56 nations. *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology*, 38(2), 173–212. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/0022022106297299

These are the two strongest empirical anchors for the question "does the Big Five work in the Global South?" Both find broad replication of factor structure across Latin American, African, South Asian, and Arab samples — but with **smaller sample sizes**, **larger response-style artifacts**, and **lower internal consistency** in sub-Saharan African and several Arab-world samples than in WEIRD samples. The trait *structure* generalizes; *mean-level comparisons* across nations remain contested.

### Cross-cultural methodology anchor

8. Triandis, H. C., & Singelis, T. M. (1998). Training to recognize individual differences in collectivism and individualism within culture. *International Journal of Intercultural Relations*, 22(1), 35–47. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/s0147-1767(97)00034-5

Critical methodological pivot: Triandis & Singelis explicitly establish that *individuals within a culture* span the I/C continuum. This is the empirical refutation of "this Latin American user must score X on collectivism because Latin America scores X on Hofstede's IDV."

## Latin American Cultural Psychology

### Mexican ethnopsychology (Diaz-Guerrero tradition)

9. Diaz-Loving, R. (1998). Contributions of Mexican ethnopsychology to the resolution of the etic-emic dilemma in personality. *Journal of Cross-Cultural Psychology*, 29(1), 104–118. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/0022022198291006
10. Diaz-Loving, R. (2005). Emergence and contributions of a Latin American indigenous social psychology. *International Journal of Psychology*, 40(4), 213–227. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207590444000168

**Note on Diaz-Guerrero (1993) "Mexican ethnopsychology"**: This is a chapter in U. Kim & J. W. Berry (Eds.), *Indigenous Psychologies: Research and Experience in Cultural Context*, Sage, 1993. Crossref searches return no individual-chapter DOI. The 1998 and 2005 Diaz-Loving papers above carry Diaz-Guerrero's program forward in DOI-verified form (Diaz-Loving was Diaz-Guerrero's student and most active English-language successor). Treat Diaz-Guerrero (1993) as a referenced foundational chapter; the Diaz-Loving DOIs are the citable anchors.

### Simpatía — Hispanic relational warmth as a cultural script

11. Triandis, H. C., Marin, G., Lisansky, J., & Betancourt, H. (1984). Simpatía as a cultural script of Hispanics. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 47(6), 1363–1375. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.47.6.1363

The foundational empirical paper. Simpatía in this measurement is operationalized as a *script* — a sequence of expected behaviors emphasizing positive social interaction, avoidance of overt conflict, and warm relational tone — not as a trait. The script applies *probabilistically* in Hispanic interactions; it is not an ethnic essence.

### Familismo — family centrality as multidimensional construct

12. Sabogal, F., Marín, G., Otero-Sabogal, R., Marín, B. V., & Pérez-Stable, E. J. (1987). Hispanic familism and acculturation: What changes and what doesn't? *Hispanic Journal of Behavioral Sciences*, 9(4), 397–412. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/07399863870094003

Sabogal et al. is the seminal psychometric operationalization. They show familismo is *multidimensional* (family obligation, family as referent, family as support) and that **some dimensions persist across acculturation** while others diminish. Critical for 2nd-Brain: do NOT use familismo as a unitary scale; surface which dimension the user is invoking.

**Note on respeto**: a paired Latin American construct (deference to elders / authority). Encyclopedia entries exist (e.g., 10.1007/978-1-4419-5659-0_659 in *Encyclopedia of Immigrant Health*) but I found no peer-reviewed psychometric scale at the empirical depth of simpatía/familismo in this curation pass. Treat respeto as a folk-cultural framing the user may invoke; mirror their language, do not assert it as established psychometric construct.

## African Indigenous Psychology

### Nsamenang — West/Central African human development

13. Nsamenang, A. B. (2006). Human ontogenesis: An indigenous African view on development and intelligence. *International Journal of Psychology*, 41(4), 293–297. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207590544000077

Nsamenang's "social ontogenesis" model proposes seven phases of development emphasizing **social responsibility and intergenerational embeddedness** rather than individual cognitive milestones. Empirically grounded in Cameroonian and broader sub-Saharan field studies. Distinguishes itself from Piagetian models by locating "intelligence" in social-functional competence, not abstract problem-solving.

**Note on Nsamenang (1992) book**: *Human Development in Cultural Context: A Third World Perspective*, Sage. Book DOI: https://doi.org/10.4135/9781483326030. Foundational monograph; cited in `knowledge_sources` for completeness even though it is a book rather than a journal paper (Crossref does carry the DOI). The 2006 *IJP* paper above is the citable empirical anchor.

### Ubuntu — African moral and relational philosophy

14. Metz, T. (2007). Toward an African moral theory. *Journal of Political Philosophy*, 15(3), 321–341. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9760.2007.00280.x

Metz is the most cited *peer-reviewed* analytic-philosophy treatment of Ubuntu. Important for 2nd-Brain: **Ubuntu is a moral-philosophical construct first, psychometric construct second**. Metz formulates "a person is a person through other persons" (umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu) as a moral principle, not an individual-difference dimension. The psychometric operationalization of Ubuntu as a measurable trait is *thin* — single-paper scales exist (e.g., the *Encyclopedia of Sciences and Religions* "Arab Indigenous Psychology" entry by analogy includes Ubuntu-adjacent operationalizations) but there is no widely validated Ubuntu inventory comparable to the K-BFI-2 for Big Five.

**Note on Mbiti (1969) book**: *African Religions and Philosophy*, Heinemann/Praeger. Foundational reference for African communal personhood; ISBN-tracked, no journal DOI. Treat as foundational background reading; Metz (2007) is the citable empirical/philosophical anchor.

**Note on theological / religion-of-Africa caution**: Ubuntu is often discussed alongside African Traditional Religion (ATR) and Christian theology. For 2nd-Brain advisor output, **never** infer that an African user holds particular religious commitments from invocation of Ubuntu language. See §Cautions §3.

## South Asian (Indian) Psychology

### Indian indigenous psychology — foundational

15. Misra, G., & Gergen, K. J. (1993). On the place of culture in psychological science. *International Journal of Psychology*, 28(2), 225–243. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207599308247186

Misra & Gergen is a seminal English-language statement of the Indian indigenous-psychology program — arguing that mainstream psychological science has treated Indian (and broader Global South) cognition as a deviation from a Western norm, rather than as a coherent system in its own right. Important for 2nd-Brain advisor framing: when an Indian user reports themes from dharma/karma framings, do NOT pathologize as fatalism — treat as a competing causal-attribution system.

### Karma as Indian theory of work / agency

16. Bhawuk, D. P. S. (2011). Karma: An Indian theory of work. In *Spirituality and Indian Psychology* (Ch. 8). Springer (International and Cultural Psychology series). Chapter DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4419-8110-3_8
17. Bhawuk, D. P. S. (2011). The global need for indigenous psychology. In *Spirituality and Indian Psychology* (Ch. 1). Springer. Chapter DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4419-8110-3_1

Bhawuk operationalizes karma as a **work-motivation and outcome-attribution framework**, not as a religious doctrine. Specifically: action orientation (karmayoga) emphasizes detachment-from-fruits, which empirically predicts different patterns of effort-outcome attribution than Western achievement-motivation frameworks. Crucial caveat: Bhawuk's operationalization draws on Hindu philosophical texts; users with non-Hindu South Asian backgrounds (Muslim, Sikh, Christian, Buddhist, Jain, atheist) may share *some* of the framing through cultural absorption without endorsing the religious basis. See §Cautions §3.

**Note on "sneh" (स्नेह — affectionate attachment in Indian family contexts)**: I found no peer-reviewed psychometric operationalization of sneh as a discrete construct at the empirical depth of jeong (Korean) or amae (Japanese) in this curation pass. The concept appears in cultural-anthropology, Indian-family-systems, and philosophical literature but lacks the operationalization (instrument, validated scale, behavioral paradigm) that would qualify for a `knowledge_sources` row. Treat sneh as a folk-cultural framing the user may invoke; mirror their language; do not assert as established psychology. [NO PEER-REVIEWED EMPIRICAL SOURCE FOUND] in this curation pass.

### Diasporic Indian / South Asian identity

18. Bhatia, S. (2002). Acculturation, dialogical voices and the construction of the diasporic self. *Theory & Psychology*, 12(1), 55–77. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/0959354302121004

Bhatia explicitly critiques Berry's (1997) four-strategy acculturation model as too clean for South Asian diaspora experience — argues that diasporic identity is constructed through *dialogical voices* (Hermans) where multiple cultural positions remain in active tension rather than resolving into "integration." Important for 2nd-Brain when serving South Asian diaspora users in US/UK/CA/AU: validate the *unresolved* dialogical position as legitimate, do not push toward Berry-style integration as a goal.

**Note on Sinha (1986) book**: *Psychology in a Third World Country: The Indian Experience*, Sage. Foundational monograph; no journal DOI. Foundational background reading; the Misra & Gergen (1993) and Bhawuk (2011) papers above carry the program forward in DOI-verified form.

**Note on Tripathi (1988) "Aligning development to values in India"**: chapter in D. Sinha & H. S. R. Kao (Eds.), *Social Values and Development: Asian Perspectives*, Sage, 1988. Crossref search returns no individual-chapter DOI. Treat as referenced foundational chapter; the citable Indian-indigenous anchors above are sufficient for the knowledge base.

## Middle Eastern / Arab Psychology

### Foundational discipline review

19. Soueif, M. I., & Ahmed, R. A. (2001). Psychology in the Arab world: Past, present, and future. *International Journal of Group Tensions*, 30(3), 211–240. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1023/a:1011653930957

Soueif & Ahmed is the most cited English-language review of Arab-region psychology as a discipline. Documents the **uneven development** of psychometric infrastructure across Arab countries (Egypt and Kuwait most developed; Maghreb and Gulf states more recently). Critical for confidence-band setting in 2nd-Brain: Arab-locale advisor outputs should carry explicit "psychometric base is moderate" tags.

### Collective personality model (Dwairy)

20. Dwairy, M. (2002). Foundations of psychosocial dynamic personality theory of collective people. *Clinical Psychology Review*, 22(3), 343–360. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/s0272-7358(01)00100-3

Dwairy formalizes a **collective-personality** model where intrapsychic conflicts (in the psychoanalytic sense) are structured around family-and-community expectations rather than individual desire-versus-superego tensions. Empirically grounded in Palestinian, Israeli-Arab, and Egyptian clinical samples. Important caveat: Dwairy's model emerged from clinical work and is most validated for *clinical* rather than *normative* populations.

### Self-assertive interdependence — empirical Arab psychology update

21. San Martin, A., Sinaceur, M., Madi, A., Tompson, S., Maddux, W. W., & Kitayama, S. (2018). Self-assertive interdependence in Arab culture. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 2(11), 830–837. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-018-0435-z

A high-impact 2018 paper showing that Arab cultural participants exhibit **both** strong interdependence (relational embeddedness, family priority) **and** high self-assertion (active voice, willingness to express disagreement) — a combination that contradicts the East-Asian "interdependence → low self-assertion" empirical pattern. Critical for 2nd-Brain: do NOT transfer East-Asian interdependence findings (e.g., Markus & Kitayama 1991, Heine 1999) to Arab users without checking the San Martin et al. distinction. Arab interdependence is structurally different from East-Asian interdependence on the self-assertion dimension.

### Islamic psychology — academic literature

**Note**: peer-reviewed academic Islamic psychology (as distinct from religious texts and devotional literature) is an emerging field. Crossref searches in this curation pass return primarily encyclopedia entries (e.g., "Psychology from an Islamic Perspective," DOI 10.1057/978-1-349-95816-0_8 in *Global Psychologies*; "Personality Psychology from an Islamic Perspective," DOI 10.1002/9781118970843.ch319 in *The Wiley Encyclopedia of Personality and Individual Differences*) and theological-integration papers (e.g., DOI 10.1007/s10943-004-4302-z in *Journal of Religion and Health*). These are reference-level rather than primary empirical studies. **2nd-Brain stance**: do not treat Islamic psychology as a single coherent framework citable as Tier-A evidence. Where a user invokes Islamic framings (e.g., tawakkul, sabr, qadar), mirror their language; do not assert these as psychometric constructs. See §Cautions §3 on religion-ethnicity conflation.

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: applicable for African and South Asian contexts via Nsamenang (2006) ontogenesis model (social responsibility milestones differ from Piagetian cognitive milestones). Less developed for Latin American and Arab contexts in this batch — defer to `attachment.md` (Strange Situation cross-cultural caveats) and Sabogal (1987) familismo as it shapes early family environment.
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: applicable — familismo conflicts intensify under acculturation pressure (Sabogal 1987); diasporic identity formation salient (Bhatia 2002); chemyon-equivalent face concerns in Arab contexts (Soueif & Ahmed 2001 documents face-management literature in Arab samples).
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: primary application window — simpatía operative in workplace and romantic contexts; karma framings of work-effort attribution (Bhawuk 2011) most active during career formation; Bhatia diasporic-self salient.
- **Adult (30–49)**: applicable — familismo / Ubuntu / Arab family-priority frames most operative when balancing nuclear family / extended family / career; Dwairy (2002) collective-personality model most applicable here as clinical baseline.
- **Midlife (50–64)**: applicable — Nsamenang's later social-ontogenesis phases (responsibility for kin, intergenerational role) come into focus; karma-as-outcome-attribution salient in life-review.
- **Elderly (65+)**: applicable — Ubuntu's intergenerational personhood (elders as ancestors-in-waiting in many sub-Saharan contexts) reframes elderly identity differently from Western generativity; cross-link `narrative-identity.md` for life-review interplay.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### When user profile = Latin American (MX / AR / BR / CO / CL / PE / VE / EC etc.)

**Weight order** (highest first, but never deterministic):

1. **Simpatía** (Triandis et al. 1984) — recognize warm-relational, conflict-avoiding interaction style as cultural script, not as conflict-avoidance pathology. Specifically: a Latin American user who softens disagreement in a journal entry about a workplace conflict is enacting script, not failing assertiveness.
2. **Familismo** (Sabogal 1987) — surface *which dimension* (obligation / referent / support) is operative. A Mexican-American user describing weekly family visits is invoking familismo-as-referent; an Argentinian user describing financial responsibility for an aging parent is invoking familismo-as-obligation. These have different advisor responses.
3. **Mexican ethnopsychology** (Diaz-Loving 1998, 2005) — the historical-philosophical orientation toward *abnegación* (self-sacrifice, particularly historically feminized) and *pasividad obediente afiliativa* (affiliative-obedient passivity) should be **recognized when surfaced by the user**, never AI-imposed. Diaz-Loving 1998 explicitly cautions against treating these as universal Mexican traits — they are *historically* situated in particular gendered and class contexts.
4. **Cross-cultural Big Five** (Schmitt 2007) — Latin American factor structure replicates well; mean-level comparisons are confounded by acquiescent response style. Use Big Five inventory results with confidence band ≥ widened by 10–15% for Latin American users.

Then layer Western frameworks (attachment, SDT) as triangulating signal.

**Brazilian-specific note**: Brazilian Portuguese cultural context overlaps substantially with Spanish-speaking Latin America on simpatía and familismo, but with distinct constructs (*jeitinho* — flexible workaround norm) that have separate empirical literature. This batch does not yet include jeitinho-specific peer-reviewed sources; future curation pass.

### When user profile = African (NG / KE / ZA / GH / ET / TZ / UG etc.)

1. **Ubuntu** (Metz 2007) — recognize community-rooted personhood claims as philosophically coherent, not as collectivist subordination. Specifically: an African user describing major decisions made in family/community council is enacting Ubuntu, not exhibiting low autonomy under SDT.
2. **Nsamenang ontogenesis** (Nsamenang 2006) — particularly relevant for lifespan-development and intelligence framings. An African user reporting "I'm not as smart as my brother" should be interpreted against social-functional intelligence (relational competence, responsibility-taking) before Western cognitive-intelligence framings.
3. **Cross-cultural Big Five** (McCrae 2005, Schmitt 2007) — sub-Saharan African samples in these papers are small (Schmitt 2007: South Africa, Ethiopia, Botswana, Morocco, DRC primarily). Factor structure replicates; *Openness* shows the weakest replication. Use Big Five inventory results with confidence band ≥ widened by 15–20% for sub-Saharan African users.
4. **Crucial limit**: "African" subsumes 1.4 billion people across 54 countries with massive linguistic, religious, and historical diversity. Ubuntu / Nsamenang most validated in Southern African (Zulu/Xhosa) and West/Central African (Cameroonian/Nigerian) contexts respectively. North African / Maghreb users overlap more with Arab/MENA frame below.

### When user profile = South Asian (IN / PK / BD / LK / NP etc.)

1. **Misra & Gergen 1993 / Bhawuk 2011 indigenizing program** — recognize that Indian psychological theory has its own internally coherent framework (rooted in dharma/karma/moksha causal-attribution structure) without imposing it.
2. **Karma as attribution framework** (Bhawuk 2011) — if a user invokes "this is my karma" framing, treat as causal-attribution claim, not as fatalism. Bhawuk shows karmayoga (action-orientation, detachment from fruits) **enhances** rather than reduces effort under stress. Do NOT translate "karma" framing into Western learned-helplessness.
3. **Sneh (folk-cultural)** — if user invokes, mirror; do not assert as construct. [NO PEER-REVIEWED EMPIRICAL SOURCE].
4. **Diasporic dialogical voices** (Bhatia 2002) — for South Asian diaspora users (≥ 60% of Indian-origin 2nd-Brain users may be diasporic given app's English-locale skew), validate unresolved bicultural position. Do not push toward Berry-style integration.
5. **Cross-cultural Big Five** (Schmitt 2007) — Indian samples (Hindi/English) replicate Big Five structure with moderate fidelity. South Asian Muslim, Sikh, Jain, Buddhist, and atheist subsamples are under-represented in cross-cultural Big Five literature. Confidence band ≥ widened by 10–15%.

**Crucial limit on religion-ethnicity conflation**: Indian ≠ Hindu. Pakistani ≠ Muslim. Sri Lankan ≠ Buddhist. The above frames must be presented as **culturally available** to South Asian users, not as belief structures they hold. See §Cautions §3.

### When user profile = Middle Eastern / Arab (EG / JO / LB / SA / MA / IQ / TN / AE etc.)

1. **Self-assertive interdependence** (San Martin et al. 2018) — recognize that an Arab user's strong family-loyalty *combined with* active voice in family / workplace decisions is the empirical Arab pattern, not a contradiction. Do NOT translate East-Asian interdependence findings here.
2. **Dwairy collective personality** (Dwairy 2002) — most relevant when user reports intrapsychic conflict involving family expectations. Frame the conflict as structurally collective (family-and-self vs. family-and-self) rather than individual-vs-society.
3. **Soueif & Ahmed 2001** — recognize the *regional psychometric infrastructure* is uneven; do not assume validated Arab-locale measurement instruments exist for every construct.
4. **Cross-cultural Big Five** — Schmitt 2007 includes Lebanon, Morocco, Turkey (Turkey is debated as Arab/MENA), and small Egyptian samples. Factor structure replicates; *Neuroticism* item interpretation differs (somatic vs psychological items load differently). Confidence band ≥ widened by 15%.
5. **Crucial limit on religion-ethnicity conflation**: Arab ≠ Muslim. Lebanese Christian, Egyptian Coptic, Jewish-Arab, secular-Arab populations are sizeable. Iranian ≠ Arab (Persian/Farsi-speaking, different historical-cultural lineage). Turkish ≠ Arab (Turkic, different linguistic family). See §Cautions §3.

### When user profile = diasporic (Latin American → US, South Asian → UK/US/CA, African → US/UK/FR, Arab → US/EU/CA)

- Apply Bhatia (2002) dialogical-voices frame for South Asian diaspora specifically; apply Berry (1997, 2005) and Benet-Martínez & Haritatos (2005) BII frame more broadly (cross-link `cross-cultural-east-asian.md`).
- Hard rule: **never** push diasporic user toward "reclaiming" a monocultural identity as more authentic. The diasporic position is legitimate, not a deficit relative to "real" cultural belonging.
- For second-generation users (born in host country to immigrant parents), the gap between *heritage culture as represented by parents* and *heritage culture as currently practiced in country of origin* is itself a meaningful psychological phenomenon. Surface, do not collapse.

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

**English (any Global South or diaspora user)**
- Tell me about a moment recently where your sense of "who you are" felt different depending on who was in the room. (Bhatia 2002; Berry / Benet-Martínez cross-link)
- When you think about a decision you made for your family that maybe you wouldn't have made just for yourself — how do you feel about it now? (familismo; Ubuntu; Dwairy collective personality — applicable broadly)
- Is there a way of thinking about why things happen (luck, destiny, karma, God's will, your own effort) that feels most natural to you? (Bhawuk 2011 karma frame opened up to user-led naming, avoids religion conflation)
- When someone disagrees with you in your family / community, how does the disagreement usually move? Who softens, who pushes, who waits? (simpatía; San Martin Arab self-assertive interdependence)

**Spanish (Latin American profile)**
- Cuéntame de una situación reciente donde sentiste que tenías que cuidar la armonía del grupo aunque sintieras algo diferente por dentro. (Triandis et al. 1984 simpatía-as-script)
- ¿En qué momentos sientes que tu familia es parte de tu propia decisión, y en qué momentos sientes que tu decisión es solo tuya? (Sabogal 1987 familismo dimensions)
- Si pudieras describir lo que tu familia espera de ti en una sola frase, ¿cuál sería? (familismo-as-obligation operationalization)

**Hindi / English-Hindi mixed (South Asian profile)**
- कोई ऐसा फैसला, जो आपने अपने परिवार के लिए लिया था और अब उसे आप कैसे देखते हैं? (familial-obligation framing, mirrors Bhawuk's karmayoga frame for action-detachment; presented in user's language without imposing religious vocabulary)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **Simpatía script active**: explicit softening of disagreement in narrative; use of *de nada*, *no te preocupes*-style language to defuse tension; difficulty surfacing complaints about close others. Do NOT code as social anxiety or low Assertiveness on Big Five facet level — check whether the user *experiences* the softening as effortful (constraint) vs natural (script).
- **Familismo dimensions**: separately tag (a) "I make decisions thinking of my family" (referent), (b) "I have to take care of my family" (obligation), (c) "My family is who I rely on" (support). Sabogal 1987 shows these are correlated but distinct.
- **Karma-as-attribution**: user invokes *karma* in English (or *karm* in Hindi/Urdu) framing of outcomes. Distinguish (a) action-orientation karmayoga ("I'll do the work, the result is not mine to control") which is empirically *adaptive* (Bhawuk 2011), from (b) fatalistic karma ("there's nothing I can do") which signals different emotional regulation pattern.
- **Ubuntu personhood framing**: user explicitly invokes "I am because we are," or family/community ancestors as moral referents, or describes self-worth through relational contribution. Tag as Ubuntu marker. Do NOT code as low self-esteem.
- **Dwairy collective-personality conflict**: user reports intrapsychic conflict where the **internalized object** is the family / community rather than an individual desire vs ought. E.g., "I want to disappoint my mother less than I want this thing for myself" — Dwairy frame, not Western superego frame.
- **San Martin self-assertive interdependence**: user reports both strong family loyalty AND active voice in family decisions. Do NOT reduce to one or the other; Arab cultural pattern.
- **Diasporic dialogical voices**: user reports "switching" between cultural selves, languages activating different selves, particular topics belonging in one cultural register but not another. Validate as Bhatia 2002 pattern.

Aggregate across ≥ 25 entries for Global South cultural-construct tagging (higher threshold than East-Asian batch because (a) surface language can be borrowed, (b) measurement infrastructure is thinner, and (c) within-region variance is large).

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- **Default**: ask the user how their cultural context interacts with the issue before applying any framework. Phrase: "Before I suggest anything, how does the [Latin American / African / South Asian / Arab] context shape this for you?" / "Estoy escuchando que esto pasa en un contexto familiar — ¿cómo lees tú la situación desde ahí?"
- **Never assert cultural belonging**: do not tell a Mexican user "as a Mexican, you probably feel X." Always offer as hypothesis the user can reject. Phrase: "This is a common pattern in [region] context — does it feel like that to you, or different?"
- **Never infer religion from cultural construct**: a South Asian user invoking karma is NOT necessarily Hindu. An Arab user invoking *sabr* (patience under hardship) is NOT necessarily Muslim. A sub-Saharan African user invoking Ubuntu is NOT necessarily Christian or ATR. See §Cautions §3 and Hard Safety Rule §0.3 in `docs/research/CLAUDE.md`.
- **Honor familismo / Ubuntu / Arab collective personality without coercing autonomy**: SDT autonomy needs (Ryan & Deci) are universal in basic-need form, but their expression is filtered through these constructs. Check the user's *felt* experience (Sheldon & Elliot 1999 self-concordance, in `values-meaning.md`) rather than the surface structure.
- **For karma-as-attribution**: if user invokes karmayoga / action-without-attachment, recognize as empirically adaptive coping (Bhawuk 2011). If user invokes fatalistic karma in distress, surface gently as a framing the user is using right now, without correcting it as "wrong attribution."
- **For diasporic users**: avoid framing one cultural side as "real self" and the other as "performed." Both are real. Bhatia (2002) frames diasporic identity as dialogically constituted, not as integration-pending.
- **For Arab users specifically**: do NOT transfer East-Asian interdependence findings (low self-assertion, harmony-seeking) to Arab interdependence (San Martin et al. 2018 explicitly shows different pattern).
- **Confidence bands**: outputs grounded in this batch should carry lower confidence than outputs grounded in the East-Asian batch or in the Western Big Five batches. Surface this to the user when relevant ("This is a pattern I've seen in some research on [region], but the research base is thinner than for some other constructs — does this resonate or not?").

## Cautions & Limitations

### 1. Essentialism risk is HIGHER for Global South than for East Asian

The East-Asian batch already warned about essentialism. For Global South constructs the risk is **more severe** because:

- The constructs are less present in mainstream personality literature, so individual studies carry more weight (no large meta-analytic counter-evidence to over-claims).
- Popular media (Western and within-region) often essentializes ("Africans are communal," "Latinos are familial," "Indians are spiritual," "Arabs are honor-bound") without empirical grounding.
- Within-region variance is enormous and under-studied (Allwood & Berry 2006 explicitly note this).

**Rule**: any construct in this batch must be presented to the user as a hypothesis they can confirm, reject, or modify. Never as established fact about them based on their nationality or region. Confidence bands ≥ widened by 10–20% relative to the East-Asian batch.

### 2. Within-culture variance > between-culture variance applies universally

The Oyserman 2002 / Schimmack 2005 finding (cross-link `cross-cultural-east-asian.md` §Cross-cultural meta-analyses) applies here as well — and arguably more strongly, since within-region variance in (e.g.) Latin America spans from rural Indigenous communities to urban professional cosmopolitan populations, often with starker socioeconomic gradients than within East Asia.

### 3. Religion-ethnicity conflation is THE central failure mode

Many Global South constructs co-occur with religious traditions: karma/Hinduism, sabr/Islam, Ubuntu/African Traditional Religion, familismo/Catholicism. **Inference rule**: from a user's cultural-construct language, NEVER infer their religion.

- Indian ≠ Hindu (≈ 80% Hindu, but 15% Muslim, 2% Christian, 2% Sikh, with significant atheist/secular subpopulations and large Buddhist, Jain populations).
- Pakistani ≠ universally observant Muslim.
- Arab ≠ Muslim. Lebanon (≈ 40% Christian), Egypt (Coptic Christian ≈ 10%), and Jewish-Arab, secular-Arab, agnostic-Arab populations are sizeable.
- Iranian ≠ Arab. Turkish ≠ Arab.
- Sub-Saharan African ≠ Christian or Muslim or ATR (all are present, often syncretic).
- Latin American ≠ Catholic. Evangelical Protestant populations growing (≈ 20% region-wide), with substantial atheist, agnostic, Afro-religious (Candomblé, Santería), and Indigenous religious populations.

This is a direct application of `computational-personality.md` §Kosinski 2013 lesson — protected categories must not be inferred from behavioral or cultural text. See `docs/research/CLAUDE.md` §0.3 Hard Safety Rule.

### 4. Diaspora ≠ in-country

Diasporic Latin American (e.g., Mexican-American 2nd generation), South Asian (e.g., British Indian 3rd generation), African (e.g., Ghanaian-American 1.5 generation), and Arab (e.g., Lebanese-French 2nd generation) users have **substantially different** psychological profiles than in-country users from the same heritage culture. The diasporic-self literature (Bhatia 2002 specifically for South Asian; Berry 1997 / Benet-Martínez 2005 cross-link more broadly) is the appropriate frame, not the monocultural constructs above.

Specifically: a 2nd-generation Mexican-American 25-year-old may have weaker familismo-as-obligation than her cousin in Guadalajara, but stronger familismo-as-referent. These are not "less authentic" Mexican-Americans — they are differently constituted.

### 5. Many constructs operationalized in only 1–2 papers

Unlike Big Five (thousands of papers) or attachment (hundreds), several constructs in this batch (Ubuntu psychometric operationalization, Nsamenang ontogenesis instrument, Bhawuk karma scale, San Martin self-assertive interdependence paradigm) rest on **a small number of empirical studies**. Treat as **operationalized hypotheses**, not Tier-A established psychology. Use Tier B classification per `docs/research/CLAUDE.md` §3.

### 6. Indigenous psychology ≠ folk-cultural framework

Per Allwood & Berry (2006): indigenous psychology is the academic-disciplinary commitment to grounding psychological theory in local conceptual and methodological resources. It is NOT the same as folk-cultural beliefs, ethnographic descriptions, or anthropological generalizations. This batch deliberately includes only sources that meet the indigenous-psychology disciplinary standard (peer-reviewed, operationalized, methodologically explicit), or that are clearly tagged as supplementary background (Diaz-Guerrero 1993 book chapter; Sinha 1986 monograph; Mbiti 1969; Tripathi 1988). Several common references in this domain (popular nonfiction, trade press, religious texts) are Tier D and excluded from `knowledge_sources` rows.

### 7. Linguistic note

Romanization and translation are inconsistent. Spanish: *simpatía* (with accent) is canonical; *simpatia* (Brazilian Portuguese) is the cognate. Arabic: *sabr* (صبر), *qadar* (قدر), *tawakkul* (توكل) — store both Latin-alphabet and Arabic-script forms. Hindi: *karma* (कर्म), *dharma* (धर्म), *sneh* (स्नेह). Sub-Saharan: *Ubuntu* (Nguni-language family); *Hunhu* (Shona); *Botho* (Sotho-Tswana). Store canonical script and romanization in `knowledge_sources.summary_en`/`summary_ko` to support retrieval regardless of which form the user uses.

### 8. Korean-locale relevance

This batch is **secondary** for Korean users (who are served primarily by `cross-cultural-east-asian.md`). However, Korean users with Global South diaspora experience (e.g., Korean-Brazilian, Korean-Mexican populations) or those serving in mission/business contexts in these regions may benefit from advisor outputs grounded in this batch. Korean-context adaptations of these constructs are **not yet established** in Korean-language academic literature in this curation pass; cross-link to existing Korean acculturation studies in `cross-cultural-east-asian.md`.

### 9. Crisis routing supersedes cultural framing

If a user's content shows crisis markers (self-harm, suicidal ideation, severe loss + acute distress), the cultural-construct layer is **deferred**. Crisis handling per `crisis-detection.md` and `crisis-detection-global.md` runs first. Cultural sensitivity is layered into crisis routing per the latter batch.
