# Framework: Personality Change Dynamics (Lifespan · Maturity · Volition · Reliable Change)

> Batch produced via `docs/research/psychology-handoff.md`. All DOIs verified against Crossref / publisher record (May 2026).
>
> This batch backs **v0.2 design doc §03 Risk 1** — "percentile changes over time" — by establishing what the literature actually says about HOW personality changes: how fast, what causes it, what realistic deltas look like, and where measurement noise ends and signal begins. The citation anchor for everything in this batch is **Bleidorn et al. (2022) Psychological Bulletin**, the most comprehensive contemporary meta of longitudinal personality stability and change.
>
> **The single most important number for the product**: natural mean-level personality change in adulthood runs ~0.1–0.2 SD per decade (Roberts, Walton & Viechtbauer 2006; Bleidorn et al. 2022). Even successful volitional intervention (Stieger et al. 2021) delivers ~0.3–0.5 SD over ~3 months — and that requires a structured chatbot program, daily exposure, and active behavioral assignments. The "percentile rank you against the population" UI must reflect this. Weekly percentile movement is almost always measurement noise.

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: not primary scope of this batch — trait structure not fully consolidated below ~10. See `big-five.md`, `erikson.md` (stages 1–4).
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: applicable — Roberts 2006 and Bleidorn 2022 both find adolescence and emerging adulthood (~10–25) show some of the largest mean-level shifts, particularly the early phase of the maturity principle.
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: applicable — period of greatest mean-level change in Conscientiousness (↑), Agreeableness (↑), and Negative Emotionality (↓). Cross-reference `emerging-adulthood.md`.
- **Adult (30–49)**: applicable — change slows but continues; life events (marriage, first job, parenting) are significant predictors (Specht 2011; Bleidorn et al. 2018).
- **Midlife (50–64)**: applicable — change becomes smaller but rank-order stability does not plateau completely; Openness shows modest decline in late midlife.
- **Elderly (65+)**: applicable — change resumes in late life on some traits (Conscientiousness may decline; Negative Emotionality may rise modestly in very late life — Roberts & Mroczek 2008; Bleidorn 2022).

## Key Findings — What the Literature Actually Says

### 1. Mean-level change is real, slow, and directional

Roberts, Walton & Viechtbauer (2006), the foundational meta of 92 longitudinal samples covering ages 10–101, established the **maturity principle**: across the life course, most adults trend toward higher Conscientiousness, higher Agreeableness, lower Negative Emotionality. Effect sizes per decade are typically **~0.1–0.2 SD** for the larger-changing traits. Bleidorn et al. (2022), a second-generation meta with newer and larger longitudinal datasets, confirms the maturity pattern with refined estimates and finds change is more continuous across the lifespan than the older "personality fixed by 30" view suggested.

### 2. Rank-order stability is high but not perfect

Even when mean-level change is small, individuals can move relative to each other. Roberts & DelVecchio (2000) — and updated by Bleidorn 2022 — find test-retest correlations rise from ~.3 in childhood to ~.7–.75 in adulthood. Implication: rank-order ("percentile against others") is not constant, but it is much more stable than week-to-week journaling noise. Most "percentile" change in short windows is measurement error, not real movement.

### 3. Reliable Change Index (Jacobson & Truax 1991)

The RCI gives a statistical threshold for whether an observed change in an individual is greater than would be expected from measurement error alone:

```
RCI = (X_post - X_pre) / SE_diff
SE_diff = SD_pre × √(2 × (1 - reliability))
```

A change is considered reliable (not noise) at |RCI| ≥ 1.96 (p < .05). For a typical Big Five scale with reliability ~.85 and SD ~0.6, this means **observed deltas smaller than ~0.5 SD between two short measurements should be treated as noise**, not real personality change. This sets a hard floor on what any "your personality has changed" UI can honestly claim.

### 4. Volitional change is real — but requires sustained intervention

- Hudson & Fraley (2015, JPSP) — the seminal demonstration: people *can* change their own traits when they want to, but only when desire is paired with successful behavioral execution.
- Hudson, Briley, Chopik & Derringer (2019, JPSP) — "you have to follow through". *Accepting* behavioral challenges did not predict trait change. Only *completing* them did. This is the central design insight for any app that promises change.
- Hudson, Fraley, Chopik & Briley (2020, SPPS) — mega-analysis of 12 intensive longitudinal studies: change goals predicted ~0.16 SD greater growth over 16 weeks for the trait participants targeted.
- **Stieger et al. (2021, PNAS)** — the most directly relevant study for 2nd-Brain. A 3-month digital chatbot intervention (PEACH) with n=1,523 produced trait changes maintained 3 months post-intervention. Effect sizes were ~0.3–0.5 SD for targeted traits when participants actively engaged. This is the realistic ceiling for what a well-designed AI-mediated reflection product can plausibly deliver.

### 5. Life events move personality — but less than folk theory expects

- Specht, Egloff & Schmukle (2011, JPSP) — the SOEP panel: marriage, divorce, first job, retirement, parenthood, death of spouse all produce measurable trait shifts, but most are modest and trait-specific (e.g., first job → Conscientiousness ↑).
- Bleidorn, Hopwood & Lucas (2018) — review/agenda paper formalizing that life events are necessary but not sufficient explanations for trait change; selection effects (who experiences which events) are substantial.
- Luhmann et al. (2021, JPSP) — life events are not unitary; the *perceived characteristics* (valence, predictability, social-status impact, etc.) matter more than the event label itself. Two people experiencing "divorce" can have opposite trajectories depending on perceived characteristics.

### 6. Individual differences in change are themselves stable

Schwaba & Bleidorn (2018) — using Dutch panel data (n≈9,636) — shows people differ systematically in *how much* they change, not just *what* they change to. The "everyone follows the same maturation curve" model is too simple; some individuals are stable, others change substantially, and this between-person variation in plasticity is itself a meaningful trait-like construct.

### 7. The sociogenomic model (Roberts 2018)

Roberts' revised sociogenomic model integrates: (a) genetic predispositions that are themselves responsive to environment ("pliable" systems), (b) state-level fluctuations that, if sustained, become trait-level change ("elastic" systems), and (c) role-based environments that maintain or shift traits over years. The practical implication: trait change happens through repeated state experience over months, not through insight or intention alone. This is why journaling alone — without sustained behavioral assignments — should be expected to produce small effects.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Realistic Change Expectations (HARD NUMBERS)

| Scenario | Expected delta | Time scale | Anchor |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Natural adult maturation, no intervention | 0.1–0.2 SD per decade per trait (Conscientiousness ↑, Negative Emotionality ↓ strongest) | 10 years | Roberts 2006; Bleidorn 2022 |
| Reliable change detectable above measurement noise | ≥ ~0.5 SD between two short measurements | depends on test-retest interval | Jacobson & Truax 1991 |
| Volitional change goal + reflection only (low fidelity) | ~0.1–0.16 SD | 16 weeks | Hudson 2020 mega-analysis |
| Volitional change with structured digital chatbot intervention (high fidelity, daily engagement) | 0.3–0.5 SD on targeted trait | 3 months | Stieger 2021 PNAS |
| Major life event (e.g., parenthood, new job) | ~0.1–0.3 SD on a relevant trait | 1–3 years | Specht 2011; Luhmann 2021 |
| Week-over-week observed change in a journaling app | **most of this is noise** | 1 week | Jacobson & Truax 1991 + Stieger 2021 |

### UI / UX rules derived from the literature

1. **Percentile rank against population must NOT update weekly.** Monthly cadence minimum, and ideally with a confidence band that visualizes measurement uncertainty. Anything faster is fabrication of motion. (Anchor: Jacobson & Truax 1991 RCI; Bleidorn 2022 — rank-order change is slow.)
2. **Trait deltas under ~0.5 SD between any two short observations should be displayed as "within measurement range / no detectable change," not as movement.** Cross-reference `computational-personality.md` Park 2015 — text-based inference has r≈0.4 ceiling, so noise floor for journal-derived estimates is even higher than for self-report inventories.
3. **The voice layer must not promise transformation.** Acceptable framing: "Personality moves slowly. Most change comes from what you do repeatedly over months, not from a single insight." Unacceptable framing: "After your insights this week, your Openness has increased," "You're becoming a new person," "Your Conscientiousness percentile jumped 12 points this month."
4. **Behavioral assignments are the active ingredient.** Per Hudson 2019 — only completed behavioral challenges predicted change. The Advisor should suggest concrete, completable behaviors (Stieger 2021's PEACH did exactly this), not just reflection prompts.
5. **Track life events separately from week-to-week mood.** Per Luhmann 2021, the perceived characteristics of an event matter more than the label. A "life event" tagged journal entry should prompt for valence, predictability, controllability — not just "what happened."
6. **Mood ≠ trait.** A single bad week is not Negative Emotionality rising. Trait estimates need ≥10 entries across ≥4 weeks before any update is even considered, and even then the update should be small.

### Interview Question Examples (validated, anchored on volitional-change literature)

**Korean — change-readiness probes (Hudson 2015/2019 style)**

- 1년 뒤의 본인을 생각하면, 본인 성격 중 어떤 부분이 좀 달라져 있으면 좋겠다고 생각하세요?
- 그 변화를 위해서 지난 한 달 동안 실제로 행동으로 옮긴 게 있다면 어떤 것이었나요?
- 큰 변화가 있던 시기를 떠올려 보면 (예: 입사, 결혼, 출산, 이직), 그때 본인의 어떤 면이 가장 많이 달라졌나요?
- 변화하려고 마음먹은 일 중에서 결국 흐지부지된 게 있다면, 어떤 점이 막혔던 것 같으세요?

**English — change-readiness probes**

- If you picture yourself a year from now, what part of your personality would you most want to be a little different?
- Over the past month, what's one concrete thing you actually *did* in service of that change — not planned, did?
- Looking back at a big transition in your life (new job, becoming a parent, a move, a loss), which part of you shifted most?
- When you've tried to change something about yourself in the past and it didn't stick, what got in the way?

### Trait Extraction Cues — Change Signal vs Noise

Cross-references `big-five.md` Trait Extraction Cues. Additional rules specific to **change detection**:

- **Do not update trait estimates more often than monthly.** Internally, the Inference engine can score continuously, but the user-facing percentile/rank surface must aggregate ≥30 days of entries.
- **Require minimum-entry threshold before any change claim**: at least 10 entries in the new window AND at least 10 in the prior window. Below this, display "still calibrating" rather than a number.
- **Flag life-event entries explicitly** (per Luhmann 2021). When the user describes a marriage, divorce, new job, loss, move, illness, or birth — capture the *perceived characteristics* (was it expected? wanted? in your control?) and treat the next 90 days of entries as a possibly-shifted regime.
- **Distinguish state from trait language**. "I've been more anxious this week" = state. "I'm an anxious person" = trait self-description. Weight trait self-descriptions higher; weight single-week state descriptions much lower.

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- **Anchor every change conversation on Bleidorn 2022 + Stieger 2021**. Be clear that meaningful change is months-to-years scale, requires sustained behavior, and even successful intervention runs ~0.3–0.5 SD — not a personality transplant.
- **Match guidance to the literature on what actually moves traits**:
  - For Conscientiousness: structural change (calendar, environment, role) more than reflection alone — Specht 2011 (first job effect), Hudson 2019 (completing challenges).
  - For Negative Emotionality: exposure-based and behavioral, not "stop worrying" — Stieger 2021 PEACH used graded behavioral exposure for Neuroticism reduction.
  - For Extraversion: behavioral assignment (more social initiation) > insight — Hudson 2019 / Hudson 2020.
  - For Openness: novel experience repetition over months — Roberts 2018 sociogenomic.
  - For Agreeableness: perspective-taking exercises + relational role engagement (caregiving, mentorship) — Caspi 2005 social-investment principle.
- **Never claim a single insight changed personality.** Acceptable: "what you noticed today is a useful piece of evidence." Unacceptable: "this realization is changing who you are."
- **When the user describes a life event**, do not rush a redemptive narrative (cross-reference `narrative-identity.md` Cautions); follow Luhmann 2021 — probe perceived characteristics first.
- **For users frustrated by lack of visible change**, normalize timelines using Roberts 2006 / Bleidorn 2022. Show the longer arc (yearly view), not the weekly view.

## Cautions & Limitations

- **Week-over-week trait change is almost always measurement noise**. Jacobson & Truax 1991 RCI sets a hard floor. The product must enforce this in the UI layer, not leave it to the LLM.
- **Mood-state contamination of trait estimation**. A user's emotional state during entry biases self-description and journal language. Stieger et al. 2021 controlled for this with multi-occasion sampling; 2nd-Brain must do similarly by averaging across days and weeks before updating any persona-card trait estimate.
- **Self-presentation drift in journals**. Per `computational-personality.md` Kosinski 2013, Schwartz 2013 — what people write is shaped by audience expectations, even when "private." This biases inferred change estimates upward when users believe the system is watching for growth.
- **Selection effects in life-event studies**. Specht 2011, Bleidorn 2018 — people who experience certain life events differ pre-event. Do not interpret a post-event trait shift as caused by the event alone.
- **Cross-cultural caveat**. Most data underlying Roberts 2006 and Bleidorn 2022 comes from WEIRD samples. The maturity principle replicates broadly but facet-level dynamics may differ in collectivist contexts. See `cross-cultural-east-asian.md`. The Korean-context gap noted above is a real limitation, not a curated choice.
- **Volitional change requires sustained engagement.** Hudson 2019: ~30% of participants who set change goals never followed through with behavior; for them, no trait change. The Advisor should not promise change without behavioral commitment.
- **No clinical claims.** Personality change ≠ symptom reduction ≠ therapy outcome. This batch concerns normal-range personality, not mental-health diagnoses. Cross-reference `ai-mental-health-safety.md`.
- **Reverse causation risk.** Some apparent personality change is actually role-based shift (Roberts 2018 sociogenomic) — the role moved, the person looks different, but the latent trait may be less changed than measurements suggest. Do not conflate role-context behavior with trait-level change.
- **Do not imply users can transform overnight.** This batch's central caution. Every UI element, every Advisor utterance, every persona-card refresh must respect months-to-years timescales.
