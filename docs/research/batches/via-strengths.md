## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

VIA identifies 24 character strengths organized under 6 virtues. **Reflection prompts only.** Do not present as a strengths assessment — direct users to the official VIA-IS for that (free at viacharacter.org).

**Korean**
- 본인이 가장 ''나답다''고 느끼는 순간은 어떤 일을 할 때예요? (Signature strength self-identification)
- 친한 사람들이 본인에 대해 ''~한 사람이지''라고 자주 말하는 게 있다면 어떤 거예요? (Observed strength)
- 본인의 강점이 오히려 본인을 힘들게 했던 적이 있다면 어떤 거였어요? (Strength overuse / underuse)
- 본인이 ''에너지를 얻는다''고 느끼는 활동과 ''소진된다''고 느끼는 활동을 구분해본다면, 어떻게 다르세요? (Vitality / zest signal)

**English**
- When do you feel most like yourself — what are you doing? (Signature strength self-identification)
- What do the people close to you most often say about you? "She's so ___" / "He's the kind of person who ___"? (Observed strength)
- Has one of your strengths ever gotten in your way? When? (Strength overuse / underuse)
- What activities give you energy, and what ones drain you? How would you describe the difference? (Vitality / zest signal)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **Signature strengths**: activities consistently described with energy, ease, and ownership across entries; "I" agency framing; loss of time-tracking ("flow") markers.
- **Strengths in use (top 5–7)**: Park, Peterson, & Seligman (2004) found hope, zest, gratitude, love, and curiosity correlate most strongly with life satisfaction. Look for these specifically in well-being-related entries.
- **Strength overuse**: stress-domain entries where a strength taken too far causes harm (curiosity → distraction; kindness → boundary collapse; perseverance → burnout).
- **Strength underuse**: vitality-domain entries where a known strength is absent from current routine.
- **Three orientations to happiness** (Peterson et al. 2005): engagement (flow), meaning (service), pleasure (positive emotion) — three independent pathways to life satisfaction. Identify which orientation dominates a user's entries.

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- Frame VIA as **identification**, not optimization. Knowing one's signature strengths is the value; "developing" them mechanically is a misuse of the framework.
- For users with strong vitality / engagement orientation: prompt around how to deploy signature strengths in domains where they're currently absent (work, relationships).
- For users with strong meaning orientation: prompt around strengths in service of larger purposes; reference Peterson et al. (2005) finding that meaning-orientation has the strongest correlation with life satisfaction.
- Avoid "all 24 strengths are equally good" flattening — McGrath (2015) shows global rank-order differences are stable and informative.
- **Do not score** the user against the VIA-IS — that requires the official instrument and licensed reporting.

## Cautions & Limitations

- **Not personality measurement**: VIA is not the Big Five and should not be conflated. It taps "values-aligned trait expression" not the dispositional traits themselves.
- **Tautology critique**: critics note that "strengths correlate with well-being" can be partly definitional — strengths are partly defined by their well-being yield. Treat VIA as a self-discovery vocabulary, not a deterministic predictor.
- **Cultural variance is real**: McGrath (2015) shows substantial cross-cultural similarity but also genuine variance in rank-orders. Korean-specific signature-strength distributions may differ from US norms; do not import US-derived "recommended strengths."
- **Strengths can be misused**: every strength has shadow forms (humor → mockery; leadership → control). Always probe overuse.
- **Korean validation pending**: no DOI-verified Korean-only VIA validation in this batch — use the 75-nation cross-cultural finding as a soft anchor, not as a Korean-specific endorsement.
- **Not clinical**: VIA does not diagnose; absence of high strengths is not pathology.
