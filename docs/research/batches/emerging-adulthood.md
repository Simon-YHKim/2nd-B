2. Côté, J. E. (2014). The dangerous myth of emerging adulthood: An evidence-based critique of a flawed developmental theory. *Applied Developmental Science*, 18(4), 177–188. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/10888691.2014.954451

**Note**: Côté is included as the principal published critique. A balanced application of this framework requires holding both views — Arnett's descriptive observation of a delayed-transition life stage and Côté's critique that the construct over-generalizes a Western middle-class trajectory and risks economic-policy harm.

## Age Range Coverage

- **Child (0–12)**: not applicable.
- **Adolescent (13–17)**: not applicable; precedes the stage.
- **Young Adult (18–29)**: applicable — this is the framework's target window.
- **Adult (30+)**: not applicable per Arnett's definition (some adapted models extend to early 30s for "established adulthood").

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Interview Question Examples (validated)

Anchored on Arnett's five features (identity exploration, instability, self-focus, feeling in-between, possibilities) plus K-IDEA's "other-focus" sixth dimension (김대희·김명식 2024). **Reflection prompts only.**

**Korean**
- 지금 본인은 ''완전한 어른''이라고 느끼시나요, 아니면 ''아직 어른은 아닌 것 같다''고 느끼시나요? 어떤 순간에 그렇게 느끼세요? (Feeling in-between)
- 지난 1–2년 동안 살던 곳, 직장, 관계 중에서 큰 변화가 있었던 게 있나요? (Instability)
- 지금 본인의 진로나 관계에 대해 ''아직 정해지지 않았다''고 느끼는 부분이 있다면 어떤 거예요? (Identity exploration)
- 만약 앞으로 5년이 본인이 원하는 대로 흘러간다면 어떤 모습이실 것 같으세요? (Possibilities)
- 요즘 본인 자신에게 집중하는 시간이 많으세요, 아니면 가족·관계에 더 집중하시나요? (Self-focus vs other-focus)

**English**
- Do you feel like you're a full adult yet, or somewhere in between? When does that come up most? (Feeling in-between)
- In the past year or two, what's changed the most — where you live, work, or who you're close to? (Instability)
- What part of your life still feels "not yet settled"? (Identity exploration)
- If the next five years went how you most hope, what would they look like? (Possibilities)
- These days, how much of your attention is on yourself vs on family or partners? (Self-focus vs other-focus)

### Trait Extraction Cues

- **High identity exploration**: explicit "trying out" / "figuring out" language; multiple job/major/relationship pivots described in non-regretful terms.
- **High instability**: frequent moves, role changes, relationship turnover; pattern of restarting plans.
- **Feeling in-between**: explicit "not yet adult" / "still feels like a kid sometimes" markers, even alongside adult responsibilities.
- **High other-focus (K-IDEA)**: family/relational obligations frame major decisions; less Western-style "self-discovery" framing. Common in Korean samples.
- **Foreclosed transition** (Côté critique angle): explicit naming of structural barriers (rent, job market, family financial expectations) blocking transition — distinguish this from "exploration."

### Advisor Guidance Patterns

- For users 18–29 in this stage: normalize the "in-between" feeling explicitly. Cite Arnett et al. (2014) finding that mental-health risks (depression, anxiety, identity confusion) are elevated in this window without indicating pathology.
- **Avoid the Côté trap**: do not frame structural constraints (un/underemployment, housing precarity, family financial pressure) as a developmental phase the user should embrace. Name the constraint when entries describe it.
- For Korean users: integrate K-IDEA's other-focus dimension. Western prompts that assume self-discovery as the central task may miss the Korean reality where 30s often arrive without the "established adulthood" milestones (independent housing, marriage, stable career) being met for structural reasons.
- For users 30+ who still self-describe as "emerging": this is increasingly common but warrants explicit conversation about what would shift the self-perception, rather than indefinitely extending the stage.

## Cautions & Limitations

- **Western middle-class default**: Arnett's original theory was based on US college students; cross-cultural and cross-class variation is substantial (Arnett et al. 2014).
- **Côté critique**: the construct risks pathologizing structural unemployment and housing crises as developmental, with potential policy harms. Hold the construct as descriptive of one trajectory, not prescriptive of a healthy norm.
- **Not for under-18 or over-30 prescription**: applying these prompts outside the 18–29 window inflates the construct beyond its evidence base.
- **Korean structural context**: Korean young adults face distinct pressures (compulsory military service for men, hyper-competitive job market, housing cost, family expectations on timing of marriage). Use the K-IDEA's "other-focus" and "instability" dimensions, not just self-focus framing.
- **Not a clinical category**: elevated distress in this stage is a risk indicator, not a diagnosis. Route distress to Safety Classifier.
