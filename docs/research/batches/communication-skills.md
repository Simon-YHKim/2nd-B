## Foundational Sources

1. Gable, S. L., Reis, H. T., Impett, E. A., & Asher, E. R. (2004). What do you do when things go right? The intrapersonal and interpersonal benefits of sharing positive events. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 87*(2), 228–245. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.87.2.228 — *Tier A.* Capitalization: active-constructive responding to a partner's GOOD news predicts relationship quality, often more than responses to bad news.

## Recent Validation (last 10 years)

1. Overall, N. C., & McNulty, J. K. (2017). What type of communication during conflict is beneficial for intimate relationships? *Current Opinion in Psychology, 13*, 1–5. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.copsyc.2016.03.002 — *Tier A.* No universally "good" conflict style: soft/positive suits minor issues; direct (even negative) engagement can be better long-term for serious problems. Fit-to-situation, not a fixed rule.

## Application to 2nd-Brain

### Interview Question Examples (validated framing)

**Korean**
- 가까운 사람이 좋은 소식을 전할 때, 보통 어떻게 반응하나요?
- 갈등이 생기면 직면하는 편인가요, 피하는 편인가요? 어느 쪽이 잘 맞았나요?

**English**
- When someone close shares good news, how do you usually respond?
- In conflict, do you tend to engage or avoid — and which has served you?

### Trait Extraction Cues
- Note the user's capitalization style (active-constructive vs passive/deflating).
- Note conflict mode (engage vs avoid) and whether it fit the issue's seriousness.

### Advisor Guidance Patterns
- Surface the good-news-response angle (Gable 2004) — often invisible to people.
- Reframe "I'm bad at conflict" as "different situations need different modes"
  (Overall & McNulty 2017), not a single skill to master.
- Mirror the user's own pattern; never prescribe a script.

## Cautions & Limitations

- Self-understanding, not couples therapy — never deliver as relationship coaching.
- Conflict-communication findings are context-dependent; avoid "always do X" advice.
- Only address the user's own behavior, never the other person's.
